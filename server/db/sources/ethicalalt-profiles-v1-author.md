# 01 — STARBUCKS

**brand_slug:** `starbucks`
**parent:** Starbucks Corporation (NASDAQ: SBUX)
**subsidiaries:** Teavana (closed), Evolution Fresh, Seattle's Best Coffee, Ethos Water
**hq_country:** US
**founded:** 1971

## Tax Record
- Effective tax rate FY2023: 24.1% (statutory 21%) — relatively standard
- Maintains offshore entities in Switzerland, UK, Netherlands for IP licensing
- Received $2.3M in state/local subsidies for new facility openings (Good Jobs First)
- No major tax fraud cases on record
- **Tax concern level: MINOR**

## Legal Record
- 2023: NLRB ruled Starbucks illegally fired union organizers at multiple locations across the US. Administrative law judges found the company violated federal labor law in stores across 20+ states. Over 400 stores had unionized by 2024 — the largest US unionization effort in decades.
- 2023: CEO Howard Schultz summoned before US Senate HELP Committee to testify about union-busting practices. Schultz refused to appear; the committee held him in contempt consideration.
- 2024: NLRB issued complaint citing 1,100+ individual labor law violations.
- Settled multiple racial bias lawsuits at store level.
- 2023: $25.6M jury verdict against Starbucks in a manager wrongful termination case (Shannon Phillips) — found to have terminated white manager to appease activist pressure rather than for documented cause.
- **Legal concern level: SIGNIFICANT (labor law violations)**

## Labor Record
- NLRB violations: 1,100+ cited violations (2024), the most by any company in recent NLRB history
- Documented firing of union organizers at Buffalo, NY stores — NLRB ordered reinstatement
- No living wage certification. Starting pay averages $15–17/hr in US (above federal minimum, below living wage in most cities)
- Supply chain: coffee sourcing operates through C.A.F.E. Practices, a proprietary self-certification system criticized for insufficient third-party verification
- Good On You: not rated (food service, not fashion)
- **Labor concern level: SIGNIFICANT**

## Environmental Record
- Single-use cup waste: billions of cups/year, majority not recyclable due to plastic lining
- "Greener Cup" pledges made in 2008 and repeatedly not met
- 2020 pledge: 50% reduction in carbon/water/waste by 2030 — 2023 progress report showed emissions increased
- EPA: no major enforcement actions on record
- **Environmental concern level: MODERATE**

## Political
- Lobbying: $1.8M (2023). Focus: labor law, food labeling, minimum wage legislation
- PAC donations: $680K (2022 cycle). Split party giving.
- Howard Schultz 2019 presidential exploration — independent/centrist platform, anti-union stance explicit in public statements

## Executive Web
- Howard Schultz (former CEO, significant shareholder): net worth ~$4B. Publicly compared union organizing to a "labor movement from the 1970s." Senate refused his request to testify privately.
- Laxman Narasimhan (current CEO): no significant outside business concerns noted

## Product Health
- Standard coffee consumption: established moderate health benefits at moderate doses
- Caloric content: many signature drinks 400–600+ calories with no meaningful nutritional value
- Sugar content: Frappuccinos contain 50–80g sugar — above WHO daily recommended limit in a single drink
- Plastic straw/lid usage: PFAS chemicals detected in some paper cup hot beverage containers (2023 testing, various sources)

## Summary
Starbucks' primary documented concern is labor law violations at scale. The company has more cited NLRB violations in recent history than any other US corporation, including documented illegal termination of union organizers. Environmental pledges have been repeatedly made and missed. Tax practices are relatively unremarkable. The founder's public resistance to unionization has been the defining corporate story of the company's recent history.

# 02 — NIKE INC.

**parent:** Nike Inc. (NYSE: NKE)
**subsidiaries:** Converse, Jordan Brand, Hurley (sold 2020)
**hq_country:** US
**founded:** 1964

- Effective tax rate FY2023: 18.4% vs 21% statutory

- IRS dispute: Nike challenged on transfer pricing of IP licenses — potential $1.4B liability (disclosed in SEC filings)
- Has received $2.0B+ in Oregon state/local tax incentives over decades (Good Jobs First)

## Legal Record
- 2018: Gender discrimination class action. Nike settled for $13M after investigation revealed systemic promotion bias against women. Senior executives resigned.
- 2019: Multiple senior male executives forced out following sexual harassment allegations and hostile workplace investigation
- 2021: Lawsuit over unpaid royalties to Black creatives (Kawhi Leonard shoe design dispute)
- Ongoing: Supply chain litigation re: Uyghur forced labor allegations in cotton sourcing
- 2023: FTC investigation into influencer disclosure practices
- Multiple patent and IP disputes annually — litigates aggressively
- **Legal concern level: MODERATE**

## Labor Record
- Good On You rating: "It's a Start" (3/5)
- Offshore manufacturing: primary production in Vietnam, Indonesia, China. Factory conditions audited but audit-gaming documented by NGOs.
- No living wage certification in supply chain
- 1990s-2000s sweatshop controversy: documented, led to current audit program. Critics note audits remain insufficient.
- US warehouse workers: documented high injury rates at distribution centers
- PFAS in athletic wear: per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances detected in Nike products in third-party testing (2022–2024)
- **Labor concern level: MODERATE**

## Environmental Record
- CDP Climate rating: B (2023) — improving trajectory
- "Move to Zero" sustainability program: scope 3 emissions from supply chain remain primary challenge
- Flyknit technology genuinely reduces waste vs. cut-and-sew
- PFAS: see above — environmental persistence concerns
- No major EPA enforcement actions
- **Environmental concern level: MINOR**

## Political
- Lobbying: $800K (2023)
- PAC: minimal direct corporate giving
- Colin Kaepernick partnership (2018): significant cultural moment, commercially successful
- Oregon HQ: benefits from state's business-friendly tax environment

## Executive Web
- Phil Knight (co-founder, major shareholder): net worth ~$38B. Knight donated $500M to University of Oregon, $400M to Stanford. No documented corruption. Significant philanthropic footprint. Politically active in Oregon education politics.
- Mark Parker / John Donahoe (recent CEOs): Donahoe criticized for misreading consumer preferences, significant stock value decline under his tenure

## Product Health
- Athletic wear: standard profile. PFAS detection in technical fabrics an emerging concern
- No product recalls of note
- Standard consumer footwear/apparel safety profile

## Summary
Nike's primary documented concerns are labor practices in offshore supply chain (persistent despite audit programs), a significant internal workplace discrimination settlement in 2018, and aggressive IP/tax strategies. It is not a clean record, but also not a record of criminal conduct. The company's sustainability claims outpace its verified progress.

- Effective global tax rate: approximately 20–22% — modest avoidance
- No major tax fraud cases on record

- No significant PAC activity (Swedish company)

- No major product recalls

## Summary
H&M is the textbook fast fashion case. The documented record shows: repeated living wage promises made and not kept, multiple greenwashing findings by European regulators, a major employee privacy violation fine, and a business model that structurally requires overproduction. The "Conscious Collection" was specifically found to be misleading by a government consumer agency and pulled from market.

# 04 — APPLE INC.

**brand_slug:** `apple`
**parent:** Apple Inc. (NASDAQ: AAPL)
**subsidiaries:** Beats Electronics, Shazam, Braeburn Capital, Intel smartphone modem division
**hq_country:** US
**founded:** 1976

## Tax Record
- Ireland structure: EU Competition Commission ruled in 2016 that Apple received illegal state aid from Ireland worth €13B — required repayment. Apple appealed; overturned by EU General Court 2020. European Court of Justice reinstated the ruling in 2024: Apple owes Ireland €13B in back taxes.
- Double Irish with Dutch Sandwich: documented offshore profit routing through Irish subsidiaries prior to 2015 OECD BEPS reforms
- Holds significant cash offshore: $30B+ in foreign subsidiaries as of recent reporting
- US effective tax rate: 14–16% in recent years vs 21% statutory
- **Tax concern level: SIGNIFICANT (€13B EU back taxes ruling)**

## Legal Record
- 2020: Settled $500M class action — admitted to deliberately slowing older iPhones via software update to manage battery degradation without disclosing this to users (the "throttlegate" scandal). Additional settlements in multiple countries totaling ~$1B globally.
- 2024: DOJ filed antitrust suit — alleges Apple illegally monopolizes smartphone market through App Store restrictions, NFC access limits, and cross-platform compatibility barriers
- EU Digital Markets Act violations (2024): Apple designated as "gatekeeper," found in violation of interoperability requirements, faces up to 10% global revenue fine
- App Store: Epic Games v. Apple (2021) — judge ruled Apple "not a monopolist" but ordered it to allow external payment links. Apple appealed.
- 2023: NLRB found Apple illegally interfered with union organizing at two retail locations
- DRC cobalt: multiple lawsuits filed against Apple (and others) for alleged complicity in child labor deaths in DRC cobalt mines supplying battery supply chain. Cases ongoing.
- **Legal concern level: SIGNIFICANT (tax ruling, antitrust, DRC litigation)**

## Labor Record
- Foxconn (primary manufacturer): 2010–2013 worker suicides at Shenzhen campus led to installation of suicide nets. Foxconn responded with pay increases and counseling programs. Conditions remain below Western standards.
- Apple Supplier Responsibility Reports: published annually, show ongoing violations of Apple's own standards (excessive overtime, underpaid recruitement fees, etc.)
- US retail workers: NLRB findings re: union interference (2023)
- Cobalt supply chain: Apple has committed to eliminating mined cobalt by 2025. Not achieved as of 2024 reporting.
- Good On You: not rated (electronics)
- **Labor concern level: MODERATE**

## Environmental Record
- Carbon neutral claim: Apple claims to be carbon neutral for corporate operations, and targets 2030 for entire product supply chain
- Product lifecycle: iPhone average replacement cycle is 2.7 years. Apple has historically made battery replacement difficult and expensive — core driver of the throttlegate litigation.
- Right to repair: Apple spent years lobbying against right-to-repair legislation. Reversed position in 2022, launched Self Service Repair program (critics note limited parts availability and high prices)
- CDP Climate rating: A- (2023) — genuine improvement trajectory
- **Environmental concern level: MODERATE (improving)**

## Political
- Lobbying: $9.4M (2023) — focus: tech regulation, antitrust, trade
- No corporate PAC. Employees make individual donations.
- Tim Cook has cultivated relationships across political spectrum; Apple's China operations create significant geopolitical sensitivity

## Executive Web
- Tim Cook: net worth ~$1.8B. Disclosed as gay (2014) — culturally significant. No documented corruption. Donates privately, minimal public political giving.
- Jony Ive (former): founded LoveFrom design firm, premium clients
- Apple's board: significant financial industry representation

## Product Health
- iPhone/iPad: standard consumer electronics EMF profile — WHO classifies as "possibly carcinogenic" (Group 2B), no confirmed causal link at normal usage
- PFAS in keyboards and some older products: documented in third-party testing. Apple moving toward elimination.
- No major product safety recalls

## Summary
Apple's primary documented concerns are: the €13B back taxes ruling (now confirmed by EU's highest court), the settled iPhone throttling case that admitted slowing devices without disclosure, the DOJ antitrust action, and the ongoing DRC cobalt child labor litigation. These are significant. Apple also has a more credible environmental trajectory than most tech companies and has made genuine supply chain improvements, though Foxconn conditions remain substandard by Western measures.

**overall_concern_level:** `significant`

Amos Hochstein: no relation. Bezos' yacht (Koru, $500M) required historic Rotterdam bridge briefly considered for removal.

## Product Health

- Alexa children's data: see legal section

Amazon's documented record combines the most worker injury citations of any major US employer, the largest corporate subsidy recipient in US history, systematic union suppression with multiple NLRB findings, two major active FTC antitrust cases, Ring surveillance without warrants, and Alexa children's data violations. Bezos's personal tax avoidance via state relocation is documented. Carbon emissions increased 40% despite high-profile climate pledges.

# 06 — NESTLÉ S.A.

**brand_slug:** `nestle`
**parent:** Nestlé S.A. (SIX: NESN)
**subsidiaries:** Nescafé, KitKat, Purina, Gerber, Stouffer's, DiGiorno, Toll House, Milo, Maggi, Dreyer's, Poland Spring, Perrier, S.Pellegrino, Coffee-Mate, Häagen-Dazs (US license only)
**hq_country:** Switzerland
**founded:** 1866

## Tax Record
- Swiss holding structure: Nestlé's domicile in Vevey benefits from Switzerland's historically favorable corporate tax rates
- Effective global tax rate: approximately 21–23% — relatively standard for a company of its structure
- Transfer pricing disputes in multiple countries
- **Tax concern level: MINOR to MODERATE**

## Legal Record
- Child labor — cocoa: Nestlé, alongside other chocolate companies, has faced litigation since 2005 over child labor in Ivory Coast cocoa supply chain. 2021: US Supreme Court ruled (8-1) plaintiffs could not sue in US courts (Nestlé USA v. Doe). The underlying child labor is not disputed — the ruling was procedural.
- Baby formula: Nestlé has faced documented violations of WHO International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes in multiple developing countries since 1970s through present. The original boycott (1977) is the longest-running consumer boycott in history. IBFAN (International Baby Food Action Network) documents ongoing violations annually.
- Water rights: bottled water operations in drought-stressed regions. 2017: Nestlé renewed a permit in San Bernardino National Forest (California, drought conditions) for $524/year to extract 36 million gallons annually. Criticized by environmental groups. California eventually revoked the permit (2021).
- 2021: Nestlé's own internal assessment (leaked by Axios) stated that 60% of its food and beverage portfolio "fails to meet a recognized definition of health."
- FDA: multiple infant formula recalls (2022 Abbott, unrelated — but highlights Gerber supply chain context)
- **Legal concern level: SIGNIFICANT (child labor, infant formula marketing, water)**

## Labor Record
- Cocoa supply chain: child labor is documented and not disputed. Nestlé has operated "Cocoa Plan" since 2009. Third-party research (Tulane University, US Department of Labor) found child labor in supply chain has not materially declined.
- Swiss headquarters workforce: standard European labor protections
- Good On You: not rated (food company, not fashion)
- **Labor concern level: SIGNIFICANT**

## Environmental Record
- Bottled water: structural opposition to municipal water infrastructure in communities where they extract
- Plastic pollution: Nestlé is consistently ranked among top global plastic polluters in annual Brand Audit (Break Free From Plastic)
- 2020 pledge: 100% recyclable or reusable packaging by 2025 — not met as of 2024
- Deforestation: palm oil sourcing linked to deforestation; Nestlé suspended several suppliers (2010 Greenpeace campaign) but issues persist
- CDP climate rating: B (2023)
- **Environmental concern level: SIGNIFICANT**

## Political
- Lobbying: active in EU food regulation, infant formula marketing rules, sugar tax debates
- Historically funded anti-regulation campaigns via industry associations

## Executive Web
- Nestlé controlled by major institutional shareholders (Vanguard, Norges Bank, etc.) plus Swiss family interests. No single dominant individual owner.
- Peter Brabeck-Letmathe (former CEO) made widely-criticized 2005 documentary statement suggesting water should be a privatized commodity rather than a public right. Statement has been frequently mischaracterized but was genuinely controversial.

## Product Health
- Ultra-processed food: the majority of Nestlé's consumer portfolio is ultra-processed. Per the company's own 2021 internal analysis: 60% fails recognized health definitions.
- Sugar content: significant across candy, beverage, and breakfast product lines
- Gerber baby food: 2021 Senate subcommittee investigation found high levels of heavy metals (arsenic, lead, cadmium, mercury) in Gerber and other baby food brands. Nestlé/Gerber dispute findings but improvements are regulatory, not voluntary.
- Infant formula: see legal section — WHO code violations in developing countries

## Summary
Nestlé's documented record covers a wide range of significant concerns: the world's most persistent baby formula marketing violations (ongoing since 1970s), documented child labor in cocoa supply chain that has not materially improved in 25 years, water extraction in drought conditions, a majority portfolio that the company itself assessed as failing health standards, and heavy metals in baby food documented by Senate investigation. The baby formula boycott — the longest consumer boycott in history — has been continuously active since 1977.
